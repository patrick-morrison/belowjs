The Timaru

Discovered by Grant Pretorius, Chas Brown, and Huon Fairbairn with Luke Nelson skippering; in a joint operation between Maritime Discovery Group and WreckSploration on 28/03/2025. The wreck lies in 50m of water off the south west of Rottnest Island.

During WWI, the Commonwealth Government requisitioned or purchased a number of dredges from around Australia, and between 1917 and 1919 brought them to WA for dredging Cockburn Sound for the construction of the Henderson Naval Base. With the cessation of the war, work stopped and the dredges were laid up in Cockburn Sound, and slowly sold off during the 1920s. One of those was the steam hopper barge 404, aka TIMARU, aka Naval Dredger 4, aka CND4, built in 1893.

A suction Hopper Dredge is a self-propelled, seagoing vessel that uses suction pipes to dredge material from the seabed and store it in a hopper. These dredgers are versatile and used for various dredging projects, including maintaining waterways, land reclamation, and coastal protection.

Identification Notes From Dr Ross Anderson (Curator, Western Australian Museum)

  • Twin-engine vessel with single stem and counter stern in 44.9m (BP) – 48m (LOA) length range
  • Suction pump dredge layout suction with suction pipe mounted on starboard side, not fishing vessel or whale-chaser
  • Twin engines have been removed/ salvaged
  • Boiler has cut-out boiler plating to salvage copper boiler tubes
  • Length of site is too short for only other suction dredges scrapped in Graveyard Governor (53.3) and Premier (51.8m)
  • Additionally no sign of any car bodies which does not fit Premier historical info, and Premier reportedly had its boiler removed
  • Parmelia is right LOA, but should exhibit a twin stem for the bucket ladder dredge that protruded through the lower bow structure
  • Site dimensions of 44.9 (BP) x 9.17m are an almost exact match for Timaru 44.19 (BP) x 9.45m allowing for variation as a result of collapsed structure and manual measurements from 3D model
  • Unusual heavy rectangular platform structure near bow is a possible crane base, in the right location when matched with historic images of Timaru
  • Site/ vessel layout differs to most steamships with boiler apparently located aft of engine, this would be possible with a single boiler and twin-screw layout
  • Site formation – Site 42 is heavily broken up, with boiler standing on end, typical of high energy sites indicating it is shallow enough to be impacted by large swells
  • Site is identified as Commonwealth Naval Dredge (CND) 4/ ex Otago Harbour Board dredge Timaru/ Lobnitz & Co yard number 404
